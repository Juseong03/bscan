# Introduction

Circular RNAs (circRNAs) are a class of covalently closed, single-stranded RNA molecules produced by a non-canonical splicing event called back-splicing, in which a downstream 5' splice site is joined to an upstream 3' splice site (Salzman et al., 2012; Jeck et al., 2013). Originally dismissed as splicing artifacts, circRNAs have since been recognized as a functionally diverse class of regulatory molecules: they act as miRNA sponges (Hansen et al., 2013), interact with RNA-binding proteins (Ashwal-Fluss et al., 2014), can be translated in a cap-independent manner (Pamudurti et al., 2017), and are increasingly exploited as disease biomarkers owing to their exceptional stability relative to linear transcripts (Meng et al., 2017; Kristensen et al., 2019). Despite rapid growth in transcriptomic profiling of circRNAs, the sequence determinants that govern which intron pairs support back-splicing remain incompletely understood, limiting the ability to predict circRNA biogenesis from genomic sequence.

The prevailing mechanistic model attributes back-splicing competence to the presence of inverted complementary sequences — most commonly ALU/SINE repeat elements — in the flanking introns of the circularized exon(s) (Jeck et al., 2013; Ivanov et al., 2015; Kramer et al., 2015). Reverse-complement pairing between upper and lower intronic ALU elements is thought to bring the two splice sites into spatial proximity, facilitating the non-canonical ligation. This "intron pairing" model predicts that the discriminative signal for back-splice site classification resides primarily in the intronic flanks, not in the exonic sequences themselves. However, whether computational models trained on BS vs. linear-splice (LS) junction datasets actually learn this intronic signal — or instead exploit easier exon-compositional shortcuts — has not been systematically evaluated.

Deep learning approaches for back-splice site prediction have evolved steadily over the past several years. DeepCircCode (Pan et al., 2019) introduced a CNN architecture operating on concatenated junction sequences. circDeep (Dang et al., 2020) incorporated conservation profiles and secondary structure features alongside sequence. JEDI (Park et al., 2021) used k-mer encoding with junction-level attention mechanisms. CircCNN (Liu et al., 2022) proposed a dual-branch CNN to separately encode the upstream and downstream junction arms. More recently, CircDC (Chen et al., 2024) proposed a dual-channel framework combining sequence and structure, and a family of CircCNN variants (CircCNN-single, CircCNN-double, CircCNN-tri; Wang et al., 2024) explored different encoding strategies for multi-arm junctions. While these approaches have progressively improved AUC on shared benchmarks, they share a common evaluation design: negatives are drawn from the same transcripts as positives, exposing models to a systematic shortcut in which back-splice junctions can be identified by exon sequence composition rather than junction-intrinsic features. As a consequence, high internal accuracy does not guarantee cross-dataset generalization, and several of these models collapse to near-random performance on external validation sets drawn from independent genomic loci.

RNA foundation models (FMs) offer a potential solution to representation-level overfitting. Pre-trained on large corpora of diverse RNA sequences using self-supervised objectives, models such as RNA-FM (Chen et al., 2022), RNAErnie (Wang et al., 2023), RNABERT (Akiyama & Sakakibara, 2022), and RNA-MSM (Zhang et al., 2024) encode nucleotide sequences into contextualized representations that integrate information across sequence families and structural contexts. These representations are learned without access to any functional annotation, making them unlikely to encode the exon-compositional correlates that supervised models exploit from biased training sets. Frozen FM embeddings — used as fixed feature extractors rather than fine-tuned — have shown promise for diverse RNA functional prediction tasks, but their value for back-splice site classification and, critically, for cross-dataset generalization has not been assessed. Concurrent work points to a broader move toward large-model representations for circRNA analysis: circ2LO (2025) combines LucaOne genomic language-model embeddings with convolution and self-attention for circRNA identification, and recent preprints apply genomic language models to circRNA detection from limited authenticated examples. These efforts target circRNA *identification* (distinguishing circRNAs from background transcripts) rather than the back-splice *site-selection* task addressed here, and none systematically evaluate the cross-dataset generalization or the exon-versus-intron representational behavior that are the focus of this study; we therefore include them as related context rather than directly comparable baselines.

Here we present **BSCAN** (Back-Splice CircRNA Attention Network), a deep learning framework that integrates frozen RNA FM embeddings with a biologically motivated three-branch architecture: (i) a 1D-CNN branch operating on FM-projected features to capture local splice-site motifs, (ii) a 2D-convolutional stem branch encoding Watson-Crick base-pair complementarity between upper and lower intronic sequences, and (iii) a cross-attention branch modeling direct interactions between the upper and lower junction representations. We evaluate BSCAN and eight published baselines on both an internal transcript-grouped benchmark and an external circAtlas-derived validation set with exon-length-matched controls, providing an unprecedented assessment of cross-dataset generalization for this class of models.

Beyond standard accuracy benchmarks, we introduce a **three-tier hard negative probing framework** to dissect what, if anything, trained models have learned about intronic pairing specificity. By systematically replacing the intronic component of BS junction sequences with introns from either linear-splice junctions (Tier 2) or other BS junctions (Tier 3), we can quantify whether models distinguish intron origin, intron type, or exon identity alone. We also investigate whether hard negative augmented training can force models to acquire intron-discriminative representations, and whether this depends on whether the encoder is a learned (one-hot/CNN) or frozen (FM) representation.

Our main findings are: (1) BSCAN-FM achieves state-of-the-art internal accuracy (AUC 0.916–0.917) with fewer trainable parameters than most baselines, with performance robust across four RNA FMs. (2) Frozen FM embeddings reduce the internal-to-external AUC gap from 39–45% (CNN-only baselines) to ~8%, demonstrating that FM representations are intrinsically less susceptible to exon-composition bias. (3) Adding a lightweight local (CNN) or sequential (Mamba) adapter on top of the frozen FM further reduces the gap to 5.3%, suggesting that local inductive bias recalibrates FM features away from distal exon signals. (4) Hard negative probing reveals a fundamental mechanistic divide: CNN-based models learn to discriminate ALU-rich BS-type from ALU-poor LS-type introns (Tier 2 AUC up to 0.73), but FM models — despite superior generalization — are complete exon classifiers that ignore intronic content entirely (Tier 2 AUC ≤ 0.57, negative T2–T3 gap). (5) Intron-pairing specificity is learnable from sequence data — hard-negative-augmented one-hot models reach Tier 3 AUC 0.84 — but FM embeddings suppress this signal structurally, remaining near-chance even under explicit hard-negative supervision. (6) Combination with thermodynamic duplex energy and de novo motif analysis provide complementary evidence of FM biological validity. Together, these findings establish BSCAN-FM as the recommended model for cross-context circRNA back-splice site prediction, while revealing a fundamental tension between exon-bias resistance and intronic signal access in current FM-based RNA models.
